SummaC (2022): https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.09525
---related:
-NLI for inconsistency detections
-running entire document at once leads to wrong evaluation if the claims are entailed
-segment documents into sentence units and aggregate scores between pairs of sentences (cross pairing sentences from answer and source)
-inconsistency detection closely related to textual entailment
-or that out-of-the-box NLI models obtain 52% accuracy at the binary classification task of inconsistency detection, only slightly above random guessing (Kryscinski et al., 2020).
    -->this is fixed with appropriate granularity (sentence level)
- In the summary inconsistency detection domain, the focus is on detecting any inconsistency, regardless of its accuracy, as prior work has shown that current automatic summarizers are predominantly inaccurate when inconsistent (Maynez et al., 2020).
----method:
-paper text and answer text split into parts (paragraphs, sentences etc.) then run each combination through the NLI model
    -SUMMACzs takes max entailment of each of those, then take the mean of the resulting 1 column vector
    -SUMMACconv take every etailment into account, calculate score from this for each (weighted in 50 intervals, so kinda just like weighted mean)

            Estimating faithfulness using LLMs The problem of detecting hallucinations in LLM generated
            responses has been extensively studied (Ji et al.,
            2023). Several authors have suggested the idea
            of predicting factuality using a few-shot prompting strategy (Zhang et al., 2023). Recent analyses, however, suggest that existing models struggle
            with detecting hallucination when using standard
            prompting strategies (Li et al., 2023; Azaria and
            Mitchell, 2023). Other approaches rely on linking
            the generated responses to facts from an external

-BARTscore: estimates factuality by looking at conditional probability of generated text given the input
-Azaria and Mitchell (2023) propose to train a supervised classifier on the weights from one of the hidden layers
    -unsuitable for proprietary LLM
-SelfCheckGPT: sample multiple answers, Annahme: correct ones are more stable
-Wang et al: 0 and 100 scale / 5-start scale ask ChatGPT to evaluate a particular aspect of a given answer (very good results)
-most evaluation relies on ground truth answer (BERTScore, moverscore) contextualized embeddings to compare similarity generated and ground truth answer, BART-score (!DIFFERNT) uses similary references to get precision and recall
---method
-1. Faithfulness: answer grounded in context, no hallucination, justification
    -claims made can be inferred from the context
    -extract set of statements (given questions and answer), for each statement yes / no (Anteil yes ist finale faithfullness score)
-2. answer relevance: answer adresses question
    - penalises incomplete, redundant
    -generate questions for the answer, take embeddings of these questions, calculate similary with original question (cos) (final score average)
-3. context relevance: context focused, containing as little irrelevant information as possible  (LLMs bad with long texts)
    - penalizes redundancy
    - extract sentences that are relevant (number extracted / number sentences in answer)

-benchmarked against GPTScore (ask chat gpt for score 1-10) and GPT ranking (select preferred answer) (MUCH BETTER than that)
--> RAGAS closely aligned with human judgements (especially faithfulness and relevance)

Evaluating the factuality of long-form text generated by large language models (LMs) is nontrivial because (1) generations often contain a mixture of supported and unsupported pieces of information, making binary judgments of quality inadequate, and (2) human evaluation is time-consuming and costly.

Juding LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench: https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685
-ROUGE, BLEU not suited for open-ended without reference answer
-LLM as a judge:
    -advantages: scalability, explainability
    -limitations: 
        - position bias (favoring first or second option)
        - verbosity bias: favoring longer answers
        - self enhancement bias (favoring own answers)
        - limited math/ reasoning capabilities
- human judgement is gold standard, but the LLM shows very close alignment with human judgement (same as agreement between 2 different human raters)
